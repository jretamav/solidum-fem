# Summary

Solidum FEM is a Python finite element library for research in computational solid mechanics. It supports linear and nonlinear static analysis, modal and transient dynamics (Newmark, Hilber--Hughes--Taylor $\alpha$, central differences), harmonic response, and seismic response-spectrum analysis (SRSS and CQC combinations). The current catalogue comprises 18 elements --- 1D structural members, 2D and 3D continuum solids, including a constant-strain triangle enriched with an embedded strong discontinuity for fracture ---, 10 material models --- linear elastic in 2D and 3D, $J_2$ plasticity in plane strain, plane stress and 3D, Drucker--Prager, isotropic damage with linear and exponential softening, traction--jump cohesive models ---, and 12 solvers --- linear, Newton--Raphson and modified Newton, cylindrical and dissipation-based arc-length continuation, modal eigensolver, Newmark and HHT-$\alpha$ in both linear and nonlinear regimes, central differences, steady-state harmonic, and response spectrum. The library is validated against canonical benchmarks of the finite element literature, including Lamé's closed-form solutions for thick-walled cylinders, the NAFEMS LE1 elliptic membrane [@NAFEMS:1990], the MacNeal--Harder slender beam [@MacNeal:1985], Bathe's wave-propagation problem [@Bathe:1996], and Hill's elastoplastic cylinder [@Hill:1950].

# Statement of need

Research in computational solid mechanics routinely produces new formulations --- return-mapping algorithms for novel yield criteria, enriched element kinematics for fracture, custom path-following solvers for snap-through and snap-back response --- that need to be implemented and quantitatively validated as part of the publication of the underlying theory. The implementation step is rarely incidental: ensuring that the code actually solves the formulation as posed, with the correct sign conventions, Voigt ordering, and trial--commit semantics for history variables, is a sizeable share of the research effort.

The need addressed by Solidum FEM is for a Python framework whose primary unit of extension is the *formulation itself* --- an element-level class with explicit shape functions, strain--displacement matrix and integration scheme, or a material-level class with explicit stress and tangent evaluation --- rather than an abstraction layer above it. The target audience is the researcher who derives a new constitutive law, a new element kinematics, or a new continuation strategy and needs to incorporate it into a working finite element kernel without rewriting the formulation to fit the framework's interfaces.

# State of the field

A mature ecosystem of open-source finite element software is available in Python. General-purpose frameworks built around the weak form of the governing partial differential equation --- FEniCSx [@Baratta:2023], its predecessor FEniCS [@Logg:2012], SfePy [@Cimrman:2019], scikit-fem [@Gustafsson:2020], GetFEM [@Renard:2020] and the more recent JAX-FEM [@Xue:2023] --- provide elegant abstractions for the discretisation of arbitrary partial differential equations and scale efficiently to large simulations. Their natural extension point, however, is the variational form: when a researcher derives a return-mapping algorithm for a new yield criterion, or an enriched element kinematics for a fracture process, the work to be done is not the assembly of a weak form but the explicit implementation of stresses, tangents and shape functions. In this regime the abstraction layer becomes friction, and the formulation tends to be reshaped to fit the framework rather than the other way around. FElupe [@Dutzler:2025] is closer in spirit, exposing element- and material-level interfaces in pure Python, but it is specialised to hyperelastic solid bodies and does not include plasticity, damage, embedded discontinuities, or nonlinear continuation solvers.

Research-oriented frameworks aimed at structural and solid mechanics --- OpenSees with its Python bindings [@McKenna:2010; @Zhu:2018] and FEAP [@Taylor:2020] --- do provide well-defined extension points for new elements and materials in the form of abstract base classes (OpenSees) or user-element and user-material subroutines (FEAP). The kernel itself, however, is not intended to be reshaped by the end-user, and modifying it requires navigating a large, design-pattern-heavy C++ or Fortran codebase whose architecture is fixed by its maintainers. This raises the entry cost whenever a new formulation does not fit the available extension contracts.

Solidum FEM occupies the gap between these two families: it is a pure Python framework whose architecture is structured so that new elements, materials and solvers can be added without modifying the core, while the formulation drives the implementation rather than being reshaped by it. Building a new package was preferred over contributing to an existing one because the design point pursued here --- a small core, a large extensible catalogue, declarative per-component contracts, and a workflow optimised for collaboration with generative AI coding assistants (see *Software design* and *Acknowledgement of generative AI use*) --- is not a feature that could be added on top of any of the frameworks above without restructuring their architecture from the ground up.

# Software design

The architectural decision that organises the rest of the library is to keep the framework's core small and the catalogue of components large and easily extensible. Each element, material and solver is a self-registering Python module: a class is annotated with a decorator (`@register_element`, `@register_material`, `@register_solver`) and an automatic discovery pass at import time populates the global registries that the YAML problem-definition layer consults. Adding a new component is therefore a local change --- a single file with a single decorator --- and requires no edits to the framework's core, the parser, or any central dispatch table.

Component behaviour is declared rather than implemented whenever possible. Each element class exposes attributes such as `STRAIN_DIM`, `DOF_NAMES` and `N_INTEGRATION_POINTS`; each material class exposes `STRAIN_DIM` together with a small contract for stress and tangent evaluation. These declarations allow the framework to validate problem definitions early --- meaningful error messages at construction time, rather than cryptic shape mismatches deep in the assembly loop --- and to dispatch generic operations (assembly of internal forces, mass matrices and geometric stiffness contributions) without per-element conditional logic. Sign and ordering conventions are fixed once at the framework level: Voigt notation with engineering shear strains, $[\varepsilon_{xx}, \varepsilon_{yy}, \gamma_{xy}]$ in two dimensions and $[\varepsilon_{xx}, \varepsilon_{yy}, \varepsilon_{zz}, \gamma_{xy}, \gamma_{yz}, \gamma_{xz}]$ in three dimensions, and a stress-resultant right-hand-rule convention for internal forces in beam and frame elements.

Two artefacts persist the design intent of the library beyond its source code. *Architecture Decision Records* (ADRs), kept as short documents in `docs/adr/`, capture the rationale for each architectural choice --- why a registry instead of explicit imports, why Voigt convention is fixed throughout, why mass density is an optional property of materials but mandatory for self-weight assembly --- so that the underlying trade-offs remain recoverable months or years later. *Specifications*, in `docs/specs/<Name>.md`, fix per component the physical formulation, the parameter list, the acceptance tests and the bibliographic references, so that each new addition is documented at the same time it is implemented. The corresponding tests --- 804 in total, including 38 dedicated validation tests against canonical benchmarks of the finite element literature --- act as the regression net for both software contracts and physical correctness.

# Research impact statement

The catalogue of Solidum FEM includes formulations from the author's research that are not commonly available as off-the-shelf components in existing Python frameworks. The embedded strong discontinuity element --- a constant-strain triangle enriched with a discontinuous displacement field condensed locally onto a cohesive traction--jump law --- derives from the author's doctoral work on the consistent embedded discontinuity formulation [@Retama:2010; @Retama:2020]. The dissipation-based arc-length continuation solver, with automatic switching between cylindrical and dissipation constraints for problems exhibiting snap-back response, implements Gutiérrez's energy-release control [@Gutierrez:2004] and its later generalisations [@Verhoosel:2009]. The time-domain integration of the dynamic equations of motion is available through the Newmark family and the Hilber--Hughes--Taylor $\alpha$ scheme [@Hilber:1977], both in linear and in nonlinear regimes.

Each component of the catalogue is validated against an analytical solution or a published benchmark. The 38 validation tests in `tests/validation/` cover Lamé's closed-form elasticity solutions for thick-walled cylinders, the NAFEMS LE1 elliptic membrane [@NAFEMS:1990], the MacNeal--Harder slender beam in its straight and curved versions [@MacNeal:1985], Bathe's wave-propagation problem [@Bathe:1996], and Hill's elastoplastic cylinder under internal pressure [@Hill:1950]. Representative results are reported in \autoref{fig:validation}. The full suite of 804 tests, exercised continuously during development, blinds the regressions in both software contracts and physical formulations and makes the catalogue suitable for use as the implementation kernel of further methodological research in computational solid mechanics.

# Acknowledgement of generative AI use

Solidum FEM has been developed through a collaborative workflow with generative AI coding assistants. The author has used Anthropic's Claude language models (Claude Sonnet and Claude Opus) accessed through the Claude Code command-line interface for the implementation of elements, materials, solvers, tests and documentation. The division of labour adopted throughout the project is the following: the author defines the physical formulations, the architectural decisions and the acceptance criteria, recording them in the project's rules document, in the Architecture Decision Records and in the per-component specifications described above; the AI implements the corresponding code; the author reviews the resulting diffs and validates the behaviour against analytical solutions and published benchmarks before each change is integrated. The author takes full responsibility for the scientific rigor, the mathematical accuracy and the physical integrity of the results. The architectural decisions described in the *Software design* section --- self-registering modules, declarative contracts, per-component specifications, persisted ADRs --- are not incidental: they are the artefacts that make this collaborative workflow viable from one session to the next, with no shared memory of past dialogue.
